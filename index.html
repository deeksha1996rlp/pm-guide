The ECCSR Framework

Values before tools.

Most PM guides start with Gantt charts. ECCSR starts with a question: how should we work? Developed by Joe Houghton at UCD Michael Smurfit, it is a values-based framework that insists ethics, collaboration, creativity, sustainability, and real results are inseparable from good project management.

70%
of projects fail without clear values alignment
5
ECCSR principles — all five must be present
2023
Year ECCSR was published by Houghton, UCD Smurfit
Key Insight
The essence of ECCSR is that project management is not a technical discipline with ethical considerations bolted on. It is an ethical discipline that uses technical tools.
Houghton, J. (2023). Project Management Made Easy: ECCSR. UCD Michael Smurfit Graduate Business School.
⚖️ E — Ethical
Ethics is the lens, not a checkbox

Every decision — procurement, data handling, funder reporting — passes through the ethical lens first. Trevino and Nelson (2021): most failures are systemic, not personal.

🤝 C — Collaborative
Co-creation, not just coordination

Edmondson (1999): psychological safety is the single strongest predictor of team performance. Build it deliberately. Sending a status update is communication. Designing a solution together is collaboration.

✨ C — Creating
Projects require creativity, not compliance

PMI PMBOK 7th Ed. (2021) shifted from rigid process to principles, recognising you cannot follow your way to a good project. You have to think your way there.

🌱 S — Sustainable
Design for the after

Elliott (2013) triple bottom line: environmental, social, economic — all simultaneously. A project that collapses when funding ends has not succeeded sustainably.

🎯 R — Results
Good intentions without delivery is not ECCSR

Houghton (2023): all values must translate into measurable outcomes for real people. The Charities Governance Code (2021) requires impact evidence, not just activity reports.

Irish Example — St. Brigid's Community Centre, Athlone

A community centre in Athlone was planning a digital literacy programme for older adults. Their first project plan was a Gantt chart and a budget spreadsheet. When they applied the ECCSR test — is this ethical, collaborative, creating value, sustainable, and delivering results? — they discovered they had never asked the participants what they actually wanted to learn. Two focus groups later, the programme was redesigned. Completion rates went from a projected 60% to a delivered 94%.

Academic Insight

Houghton (2023) argues that PM education has over-invested in technical skills and under-invested in values. ECCSR is a correction to that imbalance. You cannot separate how you work from what you produce.

ECCSR Lens — ECCSR

ECCSR is the lens itself. Every other chapter applies it to a specific domain. When any decision feels difficult, five questions: Ethical? Collaborative? Creating value? Sustainable? Results that matter?

Chapter 01 — Strategy

Strategy is what you choose not to do.

Every project exists inside a strategic context. Someone decided it mattered, that it aligned with the organisation's direction, and that it was worth the resources. But in many Irish small businesses and charities, this connection is assumed rather than examined — and the result is projects that deliver perfectly on paper while contributing nothing meaningful to where the organisation needs to go.

Key Insight
The essence of strategy is choosing what not to do. For a small Irish organisation with limited resources, every yes to a new project is a no to something else.
Porter, M.E. (1996). What is Strategy? Harvard Business Review, 74(6), 61-78.
🎯 Porter (1996)
Strategy as deliberate choice

A unique position, achieved through reinforcing activities, sustained through trade-offs competitors cannot match. Not a vision statement. Not values on a wall. A clear answer to: why this and not that.

🌊 Mintzberg (1994)
Deliberate vs emergent strategy

Deliberate strategy is your plan. Emergent strategy is the pattern that develops through real decisions. Most organisations operate with both. ECCSR asks: how do you honour both without losing direction?

🧠 Kahneman (2011)
The planning fallacy — use the outside view

Planners underestimate timelines by 20-40% even with experience. Corrective: ask what similar projects actually took for comparable organisations. Base estimates on evidence, not optimism.

📋 Strategic Test
Three questions before any project starts

1. Does this directly advance our mission? 2. Do we have realistic capacity without damaging core work? 3. Who benefits specifically and how will we know? If you cannot answer all three, the project is not ready.

Irish Example — Galway Craft Brewery

A small craft brewery in Galway was offered a contract to supply three city centre pubs. The deal would double revenue but require a second shift and a new cold store. Their strategy was artisan, local, premium. Porter's question: does saying yes require trade-offs that damage what makes us different? They said no. Six months later, one of the three pubs went bust. The strategy held because it was actually a strategy, not just an opportunity list.

Academic Insight

Porter (1996) writes that the essence of strategy is choosing what not to do. For Irish small organisations with limited resources, this is the most useful strategic insight available. Every no is as strategic as every yes.

ECCSR Lens — Strategy

Strategy without ethics is just ambition. ECCSR asks: does your strategic direction genuinely serve the people you exist to help, or does it serve the organisation's own appetite for growth and recognition? These are not always the same thing.

Chapter 02 — Planning

Where most projects are won or lost.

PMI research (2023) consistently shows that poor planning is the single most common cause of project failure — more so than lack of resources, external disruption, or bad luck. The good news is it is also the most preventable. Planning in the ECCSR framework is not bureaucratic box-ticking. It is the disciplined act of thinking through what needs to happen before it happens.

20-40%
Timeline underestimate, even with experienced planners (Kahneman, 2011)
10%
Budget contingency recommended as minimum
1
Accountable person per task in RACI — never two
1
Work Breakdown Structure — what must the project produce?

Break the project objective into major phases, then into specific deliverables, then into the tasks needed to produce each one. A WBS is a map of what is included — and what is not. Scope creep is your main enemy. A clear WBS agreed at the start is your main defence.

2
Gantt Chart — what happens when, and who is responsible?

Tasks on a timeline. Each bar shows duration and timing. Dependencies show what must finish before the next task starts. For most Irish projects, 10-20 tasks with a Red/Amber/Green status column is enough to manage well.

3
RACI Matrix — who does what, and who answers for it?

Responsible: does the work. Accountable: owns the outcome (exactly one per task). Consulted: input needed before decisions. Informed: told what happened. The most common planning failure RACI prevents: everyone thinking someone else is responsible.

4
Outside View — how long did similar projects actually take?

Kahneman (2011): the planning fallacy is predictable and correctable. Do not ask how long your plan will take. Ask what similar projects actually took for comparable organisations. Use their real timelines, not their planned ones.

5
Contingency — plan for uncertainty, not perfection

10% budget contingency and 10-15% schedule buffer are not padding. They are honest acknowledgement that uncertainty exists. The Charities Regulator Ireland recognises reasonable contingency as sound financial management.

Academic Insight

Kahneman and Tversky (1979) found that planners underestimate timelines by 20-40% even when reminded of past errors. The outside view — what did comparable projects actually take? — consistently produces more accurate estimates than inside-view planning.

ECCSR Lens — Planning

Planning collaboratively means building the plan with the people who will deliver it, not presenting them with one you built alone. Plans built with the team are more accurate, more owned, and far more likely to be followed when the pressure of delivery arrives.

Chapter 03 — Risk Management

Risk is not just what could go wrong.

ISO 31000:2018 defines risk as the effect of uncertainty on objectives. That definition is neutral — uncertainty can move outcomes in either direction. A risk that your key worker becomes unavailable is a threat. A risk that a partner offers to co-fund an expanded programme is an opportunity. David Hillson (2017): organisations that only manage threats leave value on the table.

Key Insight
A risk management process that only looks for threats is half a risk management process. Organisations that treat positive uncertainty with the same rigour as negative uncertainty consistently outperform those that do not.
Hillson, D. (2017). Managing Risk in Projects. 2nd ed. Routledge.
🎯 ISO 31000:2018
The risk matrix — likelihood x impact

Score likelihood (1-5) and impact (1-5). Multiply for a risk rating. 1-4: Low. 5-9: Medium. 10-25: High. Every risk needs a named owner and a response strategy. A risk register with no owner is a list of worries, not a management tool.

🛡️ 4 Response Strategies
Avoid, Reduce, Transfer, Accept

Avoid: change the plan to remove the risk. Reduce: lower the likelihood or impact. Transfer: move it to a third party through insurance or contract. Accept: acknowledge it and have a contingency plan ready.

🏛️ Charities Governance Code
Principle 4 — risk management is mandatory

The Charities Regulator Ireland expects all registered charities to maintain a risk register and review it regularly. Pobal, the HSE, and most local authorities require a risk register in grant applications. This is not optional.

🔒 DPC Ireland (2023)
GDPR is a project risk

The Data Protection Commission has flagged increasing breaches in the community sector: staff using personal email for beneficiary data, unsecured spreadsheets, AI tools without data processing agreements. Include GDPR in every risk register.

Irish Example — Limerick Sports Club — Dormant Accounts Application

A Limerick GAA club applied to the Dormant Accounts Fund for a new community pavilion listing one risk: construction delays. The Pobal assessor asked about single-funder dependency, planning permission risk, and key-person dependency (one volunteer was managing the entire build). None had been considered. The application was returned for revision. The risk register built for compliance had failed them at exactly the moment they needed it most.

Academic Insight

Hillson (2017) argues that risk and opportunity are two sides of the same coin. A risk management process that only looks for threats is half a process. The organisations that treat positive uncertainty with the same rigour consistently outperform those that do not.

ECCSR Lens — Risk

Ethical risk management asks first: what is the risk to the people this project is meant to serve if it fails? In a small Irish charity, those stakes are personal. Risk management is an act of care for beneficiaries, not a compliance task for funders.

Chapter 04 — Teams

What hospital teams teach us about project performance.

Projects are delivered by people. Everything else — templates, methodology, risk registers — exists to help people work together effectively. Yet team dynamics consistently receive less attention than schedules and budgets. For small Irish organisations where the team might be two paid staff, four volunteers, and a board member giving fifteen hours a week, getting the human side right is everything.

🏗️ Tuckman (1965)
Forming, Storming, Norming, Performing

Forming: polite and cautious. Storming: real disagreements surface — necessary, not a problem. Norming: the team finds its rhythm. Performing: energy goes into the work. For projects under 12 weeks, a team may never reach Performing. Plan for this.

🛡️ Edmondson (1999)
The best teams surface the most errors

Harvard research found the best teams reported MORE errors, not fewer — because they had psychological safety. The belief that you can speak up without punishment is the single strongest predictor of team performance.

🎭 Belbin (2010)
Nine roles — which ones are missing?

Plant, Resource Investigator, Co-ordinator, Shaper, Monitor Evaluator, Teamworker, Implementer, Completer Finisher, Specialist. A team of all Shapers will generate energy but struggle to finish anything. Know what your team is missing.

❤️ Herzberg (1966)
Volunteers need motivators, not hygiene factors

Hygiene factors (pay, conditions) prevent dissatisfaction but do not create motivation. Volunteers have no pay — their entire motivation comes from meaningful work, recognition, and growth. Managing volunteers like employees is a reliable way to lose them.

Psychological Safety in Practice
The project manager sets the psychological safety climate through daily behaviour. Ask questions instead of giving answers. Acknowledge your own mistakes openly. Respond genuinely when someone raises a concern. Protect the people who speak up.
Edmondson, A. (1999). Psychological Safety and Learning Behaviour in Work Teams. Administrative Science Quarterly, 44(2), 350-383.
Irish Example — Waterford Social Enterprise

A Waterford social enterprise brought three new volunteers into a project team mid-delivery. Within two weeks, two of the original paid staff had stopped raising concerns in meetings. The project manager assumed everything was fine because no one was complaining. Edmondson would recognise this immediately: the team had gone back to Forming. Three one-to-one conversations in the next week surfaced three significant risks that had been known but not shared.

Academic Insight

Edmondson (1999) found that the best-performing teams were not those that made the fewest mistakes — they were the ones that noticed and talked about their mistakes honestly. Psychological safety is what makes that possible.

ECCSR Lens — Teams

Collaborative teams do not happen by accident. ECCSR requires that psychological safety, clear roles, and genuine co-creation be built deliberately into how a team works from the very start of every project.

Chapter 05 — Leadership

Management handles complexity. Leadership handles change.

John Kotter (1996) draws a distinction every project manager needs: management and leadership are different functions. Management keeps the organisation running — planning, budgeting, monitoring. Leadership creates conditions for change — establishing direction, bringing people with you. Projects are fundamentally about change. They require leadership, not just management.

🔄 Kotter (1996)
The management-leadership split

Management handles complexity. Leadership handles change. Projects are change initiatives. The mistake is thinking that doing the management work well is the same as leading. Most project managers over-manage and under-lead.

🎯 Hersey & Blanchard (1969)
Four leadership styles — choose deliberately

Directing: new to the task, needs close oversight. Coaching: developing skill, needs encouragement. Supporting: capable but needs confidence. Delegating: skilled and motivated, trust them. Same person needs different styles for different tasks.

🤲 Greenleaf (1977)
Servant leadership — remove obstacles, build capacity

The leader's primary role is to serve the people they lead. Greenleaf's test: do those being led grow? Become more capable, more confident, more likely to lead themselves? For Irish community organisations, servant leadership is not idealistic — it is structurally coherent.

💡 Goleman (1995)
Emotional intelligence outpredicts IQ

Self-awareness, self-regulation, motivation, empathy, social skill — stronger predictors of leadership effectiveness than cognitive intelligence or technical expertise. For project managers under pressure, reading a room is a core operational competency.

Academic Insight

Kotter (1996) found that organisations consistently over-manage and under-lead during change initiatives. Projects are change initiatives. The project manager sets the leadership climate whether they think of themselves as a leader or not.

ECCSR Lens — Leadership

Leadership without ethics is just authority. ECCSR requires that the influence a project leader holds is used in service of the mission and the people the project is meant to help — not in service of their own comfort, status, or convenience.

Chapter 06 — Stakeholders

Everyone is a stakeholder. Including the people you forgot.

Edward Freeman (1984) defined a stakeholder as anyone who affects or is affected by the achievement of an organisation's objectives. The most common stakeholder failure in small Irish organisations is not managing stakeholders badly. It is failing to recognise some of them as stakeholders at all. Beneficiaries — the people the project exists to serve — are the most frequently overlooked group.

💎 High Power · High Interest
Manage Closely

Regular personal engagement, genuine involvement in key decisions, consistent communication. For most Irish charity projects: primary funder, CEO, key beneficiary representatives. Missing this group is the most common cause of late-stage project failure.

🎖️ High Power · Low Interest
Keep Satisfied

Strategic updates focused on outcomes, not detail. Too much information frustrates them. Too little creates the risk they use their power negatively. Get the frequency and depth right — this is where most project managers pitch too high or too low.

📣 Low Power · High Interest
Keep Informed — often your loudest advocates

These stakeholders care deeply but cannot directly affect the project. They are frequently your most vocal advocates — or, if ignored, your most vocal critics. Beneficiaries often sit here. The Charities Governance Code (2021) requires their voice be actively sought.

👁️ Low Power · Low Interest
Monitor — watch for position changes

Minimal active management, but check regularly whether their power or interest has shifted. A local councillor who was low-interest can become high-power very quickly if the project creates a community relations issue. Early detection is the value.

Irish Example — Donegal Craft Business — LEO Innovation Voucher

A Donegal craft business received an LEO Innovation Voucher to build an e-commerce site. They mapped three stakeholders: themselves, the web developer, and the LEO programme officer. When the site launched, their existing wholesale stockists — who had not been mapped as stakeholders — felt blindsided by direct-to-consumer competition. Three stocking relationships ended. Freeman's stakeholder definition would have caught this: the stockists were clearly affected by the achievement of the organisation's objectives.

Academic Insight

Freeman (1984) argued that organisations focused solely on the interests of their funders or owners were both ethically insufficient and strategically unwise. The research supports this: organisations that genuinely serve all their stakeholders consistently outperform those that serve only the most powerful.

ECCSR Lens — Stakeholders

Truly collaborative project management means genuine stakeholder engagement — not consultation as a box-ticking exercise, but co-design as a working practice. The question to ask honestly is: who should be in the room that currently is not?

Chapter 07 — Ethics

Most ethical failures are not caused by bad people.

Trevino and Nelson (2021) have studied organisational ethics for decades. Their conclusion is consistent: most ethical failures are caused by ordinary people responding to systems and cultures that make compromise feel normal, necessary, or even virtuous. Projects under delivery pressure are particularly vulnerable. ECCSR puts ethics first because without it, the other four principles are aspirations.

Ethical fade — how standards erode gradually Trevino & Nelson, 2021 ▼
Ethical fade is the gradual erosion of standards through a series of small compromises that each seem minor at the time. A project manager adjusts a timesheet entry. A charity director handles a safeguarding concern informally. A fundraiser slightly exaggerates projected impact. None of these people think of themselves as acting unethically. All of them are responding to pressures that made the compromise feel justified. Projects under deadline pressure are especially vulnerable.
GDPR in practice for Irish organisations DPC Ireland, 2023 ▼
The Data Protection Commission Ireland (2023) has flagged consistent breach patterns in the community sector: staff using personal email for sensitive data, storing beneficiary information in unsecured spreadsheets, using AI tools with personal data without proper data processing agreements, and retaining data longer than necessary. A data protection impact assessment should be completed for any project touching sensitive personal data before that project begins. The fine for serious breaches is up to 4% of annual global turnover.
The APM Code of Professional Conduct APM, 2021 ▼
Four core principles: Integrity (honest and trustworthy in all dealings), Respect (valuing the dignity and rights of all people), Accountability (owning the outcomes of your decisions), and Competence (only undertaking work you are qualified to do). The accountability principle matters most in small Irish organisations where oversight may be limited. When no one is watching, what standards do you apply? ECCSR's answer: the same ones.
Conflicts of interest — declare and step back Charities Governance Code, 2021 ▼
A conflict of interest arises when private interests could influence professional decisions. The Charities Governance Code requires all trustees and staff to declare conflicts and step back from affected decisions. In procurement, personnel decisions, and reporting, conflicts arise regularly. The standard is simple: declare it and step out. The organisations that handle this consistently and transparently are the ones that maintain trust with funders and regulators.
Supply chain ethics and EU due diligence EU CSDD Directive, 2024 ▼
Ethical project management extends to the supply chain. The EU Corporate Sustainability Due Diligence Directive (2024) will require organisations above certain thresholds to conduct human rights and environmental due diligence in their supply chains. Even below those thresholds, ECCSR requires that procurement decisions reflect the organisation's values. Who are you buying from, and under what conditions do their employees work?
Academic Insight

Trevino and Nelson (2021) identify ethical fade as the most common route to organisational misconduct. It is a gradual slide through individually small compromises. Projects under pressure are especially vulnerable, which is precisely why explicit ethical commitments matter most when resources are tight.

ECCSR Lens — Ethics

The E in ECCSR is not a starting point you move past. It applies to every decision, every day, throughout the entire project. Ethics is not the first stage. It is the condition that makes all the other stages possible.

Chapter 08 — Sustainability

What will remain when the funding ends?

A project that delivers excellent outcomes for three years and collapses when the funding ends has not succeeded sustainably. This pattern is common in the Irish community sector and it is preventable — if sustainability is built into project design from the beginning. Elliott (2013) describes the triple bottom line: environmental, social, and economic sustainability, all simultaneously.

🌍 Elliott (2013) — Triple Bottom Line
Environmental sustainability

What impact does the project have on the natural world? Carbon footprint, waste, resource use, local biodiversity. The APM Sustainability Working Group (2021) argues sustainability should be integrated into every phase of delivery, not added as a reporting requirement at the end.

👥 Social Sustainability
Impact on people and communities

What does the project do to people? Wellbeing of beneficiaries, working conditions of staff and volunteers, community relationships, equity and inclusion. Does it build the capacity of the people it serves, or create dependency?

💰 Economic Sustainability
Does it create lasting value?

The most neglected dimension in Irish charities. Does the project leave the organisation stronger or weaker? Does it create capacity that persists, or consume resources without building anything? A service that collapses when funding ends has not created economic sustainability.

📊 Impact Measurement
Outputs, outcomes, and impact — know the difference

Pobal, Community Foundation for Ireland, and Rethink Ireland now require impact evidence, not just activity. Activities: what you did. Outputs: what you produced. Outcomes: what changed. Impact: the lasting difference. Build measurement in before delivery starts, not after.

Academic Insight

Elliott (2013) argues that sustainability is not a constraint on project management — it is a framework for making better decisions. Projects designed with sustainability in mind from the outset tend to be more durable, more supported by the communities they serve, and more resilient when circumstances change.

ECCSR Lens — Sustainability

The S in ECCSR is about the fundamental question of whether the results being created are worth creating and whether they will last beyond the funding. Short-term delivery at long-term cost is not success.

Chapter 09 — Governance

Governance is not bureaucracy. It's how trust is built.

Governance is how an organisation is directed and controlled — who has authority to make which decisions, how accountability is exercised, and how the organisation demonstrates trustworthiness. For Irish registered charities, governance is a legal requirement. The Charities Governance Code (Ireland, 2021) sets out what must be demonstrated, and the Charities Regulator has the power to investigate and take action against non-compliant organisations.

1
Principle 1 — Advancing Charitable Purpose

The charity must have a clear charitable purpose and demonstrate that every project advances it. Every project must connect clearly to the stated mission. If it does not, you need to be asking why you are doing it.

2
Principle 2 — Behaving with Integrity

Honest and transparent conduct, a conflicts of interest policy, and resources used solely for charitable purposes. For project managers: declare conflicts and step back from affected decisions, every time.

3
Principle 3 — Leading People

The right people in the right roles with the right culture. For projects: ensure board members with relevant expertise are appropriately involved in major project decisions, not just informed after the fact.

4
Principle 4 — Exercising Control

Financial controls, risk management, and board oversight. This is the principle most directly relevant to project management. The Charities Regulator expects to see a risk register being actively maintained and reviewed.

5
Principle 5 — Working Effectively

The board functions well as a group with clear roles. A project sponsor — a board member with ultimate accountability for a project — is the structural link between board governance and project delivery.

6
Principle 6 — Being Accountable and Transparent

Annual reports, audited accounts, public benefit statements, and genuine beneficiary engagement. Pobal, Community Foundation for Ireland, and the Dormant Accounts Fund all conduct governance assessments before funding decisions.

Academic Insight

APM (2019) found that organisations with clear project governance structures are significantly more likely to deliver on time and within budget. Governance is a performance tool, not just a compliance requirement.

ECCSR Lens — Governance

Governance without genuine ethical commitment is compliance theatre. ECCSR asks: do your governance structures actually hold the organisation accountable to its mission and its beneficiaries, or do they exist primarily to satisfy external requirements?

Chapter 10 — Methodologies

The right methodology is the one that fits your project.

PMI's PMBOK 7th Edition (2021) explicitly moved away from prescribing a specific methodology to articulating principles that should guide project work regardless of method. The best methodology is the one that fits the specific project, team, and context — not the one that is fashionable, or the one a consultant prefers.

Key Insight
For most small Irish organisations, a deliberate hybrid works best: Waterfall-style planning and governance combined with Agile-style execution. This gives funders the structured reporting they need while preserving the adaptability real-world delivery demands.
Synthesised from PMI PMBOK 7th Ed. (2021) and practitioner guidance for Irish SME context.
💧 Waterfall
Sequential phases — know your scope upfront

Each phase completes before the next begins. Works when requirements are clear and stable. Construction, procurement, equipment installation. Less effective when the right solution needs to be discovered through iteration.

🔄 Agile — Beck et al. (2001)
Iterative cycles — respond to change

Short sprints, continuous feedback, working increments. Brilliant for programme development, digital projects, service design. The tension for Irish organisations: most funders require detailed advance planning that Agile resists.

👑 PRINCE2
Governance-heavy — strong for public sector

Seven principles, seven themes, seven processes. Strong stage-gate governance. For most small Irish charities, full PRINCE2 is disproportionate — but the principles (continued justification, defined roles, manage by exception) offer useful guidance.

🔀 Hybrid — recommended for Irish teams
Waterfall planning + Agile execution

Structured reporting for funders. Adaptive delivery for real-world conditions. This is what most experienced Irish project managers already do instinctively. Knowing the frameworks makes the choices deliberate rather than accidental.

Chapter 12 — Implementation

Plans do not deliver themselves.

The transition from a well-structured plan to actual delivery is where most project failures occur — not because the plan was wrong, but because implementation requires a different set of skills. Managing people through change. Keeping momentum over weeks and months. Responding to obstacles no one predicted. Keeping the team aligned as reality diverges from the original picture.

Kotter's Key Finding
Declaring victory too soon is the single most common failure in change initiatives. Short-term improvements create complacency that allows old habits to reassert. Sustained implementation requires sustained leadership attention throughout — not just at the beginning.
Kotter, J.P. (1996). Leading Change. Harvard Business School Press.
1
Step 1 — Create Urgency

People will not change without a genuine shared understanding of why the current situation is inadequate and why acting now matters. Not panic — real urgency based on real evidence. Without it, people return to existing priorities.

2
Step 2 — Build a Guiding Coalition

No project manager can implement significant change alone. You need people with the right authority, expertise, credibility, and commitment. For a small Irish charity, this might be four or five people — including at least one from the community being served.

3
Step 3 — Develop and Communicate the Vision

Make the change feel worthwhile in three minutes or less. Then communicate it relentlessly — through words and through behaviour. People watch what leaders do, not just what they say.

4
Step 4 — Empower Action by Removing Obstacles

Structural, skills-based, or human. The guiding coalition must be willing to address obstacles directly, including the difficult people who are quietly undermining progress.

5
Steps 5 and 6 — Short-Term Wins, then Consolidate

Plan visible improvements every 6-8 weeks. These build momentum and neutralise scepticism. Then use the credibility earned to tackle bigger challenges. Do not declare victory too early — Kotter found this is the single most common failure.

6
Steps 7 and 8 — Anchor in Culture and Run a Retrospective

New approaches must become embedded in norms and stories. Then hold a 90-minute retrospective with the whole team: what worked, what did not, what would we do differently? The value is in applying the answers, not just having the conversation.

Irish Example — Meath County Council-Funded Community Project

A Meath County Council-funded community project introduced a new digital referral system. The project manager ran a two-hour training session and assumed that was Step 5 done. Three months later, adoption was at 20%. Two senior community workers had been quietly telling new referrers the old phone system was "still fine." Kotter's guiding coalition had a gap: no one in that senior cohort had been brought in early. When she ran Step 2 properly — built a coalition that included those two workers — adoption reached 85% in six weeks.

Academic Insight

Kotter (1996) found that the single most common error in major change efforts was declaring victory too soon. Sustained implementation requires sustained leadership attention throughout, not just at the beginning.

ECCSR Lens — Implementation

How you implement matters as much as what you deliver. ECCSR asks: were the people affected by the change genuinely listened to? Were their concerns addressed? Do the results serve the mission as intended?

Chapter 13 — Decision Making & Negotiations

Every project is a sequence of negotiations.

Every project is a continuous sequence of decisions, and every significant decision involves negotiation — whether you name it that way or not. When you agree scope with a funder, that is a negotiation. When you allocate limited budget across competing priorities, that is a decision and a negotiation simultaneously. Fisher and Ury (1991): negotiation is not a battle to be won. It is joint decision-making between parties with different interests.

🧠 Kahneman (2011) — System 1 vs 2
Most decisions are intuitions rationalised after the fact

System 1 is fast, intuitive, automatic. System 2 is slow, analytical, deliberate. Most decisions that feel rational are actually System 1 intuitions that System 2 rationalises afterwards. Significant project decisions benefit from deliberately slowing down.

🎯 Fisher & Ury (1991)
Interests vs positions — the key distinction

A position is what someone says they want. An interest is why they want it. Positions are often incompatible; interests often are not. A funder requiring weekly reporting (position) may simply need assurance funds are being used correctly (interest) — which can be met many other ways.

💪 BATNA
Your best alternative is your negotiating power

Best Alternative to a Negotiated Agreement: the course of action you take if this negotiation fails. The stronger your BATNA, the stronger your position. Building a strong BATNA — diversified funding, reserves, alternative delivery models — directly strengthens every funder negotiation.

⚠️ Cognitive Biases
Four traps that derail Irish organisations

Anchoring: first number sets the frame. Groupthink: harmony overrides honest assessment. Sunk cost fallacy: past investment drives current decisions irrationally. Overconfidence: we consistently predict better outcomes than base rates support. All four are predictable and correctable.

🔄 Thomas & Kilmann (1974)
Five conflict styles — choose deliberately

Competing (win/lose), Accommodating (lose/win), Avoiding, Collaborating (win/win), Compromising. No style is universally right. The most effective negotiators choose based on the situation, not from habit or discomfort with conflict.

📋 DECIDE Model
A structured path through complex choices

Define the problem. Establish criteria. Consider alternatives. Identify best option. Develop an action plan. Evaluate the outcome. For significant project decisions, working through DECIDE takes 30-60 minutes and consistently produces better outcomes than intuitive decision-making alone.

Academic Insight

Fisher and Ury (1991) found that the most common negotiating mistake is confusing a person's position with their interest. Interests are almost always more flexible than positions — surfacing the underlying interest opens possibilities that positional bargaining consistently closes off.

ECCSR Lens — Decisions

Every decision-making process should pass the ECCSR test: is it Ethical and transparent? Is it Collaborative with the right voices heard? Does it Create value? Is it Sustainable in its consequences? And does it deliver Results that matter to the people being served?
